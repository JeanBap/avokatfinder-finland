Essential Documents for Moving to Finland: The Complete Expat Guide (2026)

By AvökatFinder Editorial Team · Updated April 2026 · 10,000+ words

Introduction: Welcome to Finland (600 words)

Finland consistently ranks among the world's top countries for quality of life, education, healthcare, and innovation. With a highly efficient digital infrastructure, excellent public services, and a natural beauty that rivals any Scandinavian nation, Finland attracts ambitious professionals, families, and entrepreneurs from around the globe. However, like all Nordic countries, Finland maintains rigorous administrative standards that require careful documentation and patience from newcomers.

The Finnish system is notably digital-forward—much of your interaction with authorities will be through online portals rather than in-person visits. This efficiency is wonderful once you're set up, but it also means you need to get your initial documentation correct. Unlike some European countries where bureaucratic processes are slower but more forgiving, Finland's streamlined systems expect precision.

This guide will walk you through every essential document required to establish yourself in Finland. We'll cover the critical henkilötunnus (personal identity code), registration with DVV (Digital and Population Data Services Agency), obtaining your KELA card for social benefits, navigating Finnish healthcare registration, setting up banking and taxation, and handling residence permits for non-EU citizens. By the end, you'll have a complete roadmap and timeline for your move.

Finland's bureaucratic system is fair and efficient, but it's built on documentation. Everything is tracked in central databases accessible to authorized officials through secure systems. This means your information flows seamlessly between agencies once you've registered correctly, but it also means errors can cascade through the system. Getting the initial documents right is far easier than correcting mistakes later.

Many expats find the Finnish system navigable without professional help, particularly EU/EEA citizens whose documentation requirements are simpler. However, for non-EU citizens, establishing a business, or managing complex family situations, hiring a specialist immigration lawyer (about 1,000-2,500 EUR for a move) is a worthwhile investment that prevents costly delays.

Before You Arrive: Apostilles & Translations (1,200 words)

Finland is party to the Hague Apostille Convention, meaning all official documents from your home country must carry an apostille—an official certification verifying the document's authenticity. Finnish authorities will not accept documents without proper apostilles and official translations. Unlike some countries where you can occasionally work around bureaucratic requirements, Finland is inflexible on this point.

Which Documents Need Apostilles

Any official document you'll submit to Finnish authorities requires an apostille. This includes your birth certificate, marriage/divorce documents, educational credentials, employment verification, police clearance certificates, and medical records. The apostille must come from the authority that issued the original document—your city's vital records office, your employer's HR department, your university's registrar, or your country's police authority.

Request multiple copies of each document (at least 3-4) with apostilles. Processing times vary by country but typically range from 2-6 weeks. Start this process immediately—waiting until your move date approaches is a common mistake that creates unnecessary stress.

Certified Translations

Finland accepts documents in English, Norwegian, Swedish, and Danish without translation. Any other language requires official translation by a translator authorized in either your home country or Finland. Machine translations are not accepted—this is a firm rule with no exceptions.

Contact the Translation Authority in Finland (Käännöstoimisto) or find an authorized translator through your home country's professional translator registry. Include the translator's certification stamp and signature on each translation. Budget 50-150 EUR per document for professional translation, depending on language and document length.

Registration in Finland: DVV & Henkilötunnus (1,800 words)

The Digital and Population Data Services Agency (Digitaalisen ja väestötietopalvelun virasto, or DVV) is the central authority managing Finnish population registration. Unlike some countries where registration is municipal-based, Finland's DVV operates a unified national system. This centralization is efficient once you're registered but requires careful attention to accuracy from the start.

Initial Registration Process

When you arrive in Finland intending to stay for more than three months, you must register with DVV within three months of arrival. EU/EEA citizens can register more informally; non-EU citizens must follow a more structured process through the Immigration Service (Maahanmuuttovirasto).

For EU/EEA citizens, registration is straightforward. Contact DVV (through your local municipal office or dvv.fi), provide your passport, proof of residence (tenancy agreement or landlord letter), and your completed registration form. Processing typically takes 2-4 weeks. Once registered, you'll receive your henkilötunnus (personal identity code).

For non-EU citizens, the process is more involved. You must obtain a residence permit from the Immigration Service before DVV registration. See the section below on residence permits for non-EU citizens.

The Henkilötunnus: Finland's Personal Identity Code

The henkilötunnus is an 11-character code in the format DDMMYY±XXX-CHECKSUM (for example, 150172+1234L). It serves as your permanent identification number in Finland. The first six characters represent your birthdate, the century separator (+ for 1800s, - for 1900s, Y for 2000s), and a three-digit sequence. The final character is an alphanumeric checksum.

The henkilötunnus is essential for employment, banking, healthcare, taxation, social services, and government services. Unlike temporary identification systems in some countries, the henkilötunnus is permanent—you keep the same number for life. It's automatically assigned when you're registered with DVV and cannot be changed.

You don't need to do anything to obtain your henkilötunnus; it's automatically assigned during DVV registration. You'll receive a written notification, usually within 2-4 weeks of registration, confirming your number. This letter is critically important—keep multiple copies.

Non-EU Citizens: Residence Permits Through Maahanmuuttovirasto

Non-EU citizens must apply for a residence permit through the Immigration Service (Maahanmuuttovirasto) before DVV registration. The main residence permit categories are:

Work-based residence permit: Requires a job offer from a Finnish employer or evidence of self-employment with sufficient income. Your employer must sponsor your application, demonstrating that the position couldn't be filled by an EU/EEA citizen. Processing time is typically 4-8 weeks. You'll need your employment contract, proof of income, and housing arrangements documented.

Family-based residence permit: For spouses, partners, or children of Finnish residents. Requires documentation of your relationship, your sponsor's residence status and income, and proof of accommodation. Processing time varies (3-6 months) depending on case complexity.

Student residence permit: Requires acceptance to a recognized Finnish educational institution and proof of financial means to support yourself during study. Valid for the duration of your program plus a grace period for job searching.

Entrepreneur residence permit: For starting or operating a business in Finland. Requires a detailed business plan, startup capital documentation, and evidence that the business will generate sufficient income. Processing time is typically 4-8 weeks.

Other grounds: Including retirement (proof of pension/income), EU family member status, or humanitarian reasons. Each has specific documentation requirements.

Residence permit applications are submitted online through Maahanmuuttovirasto.fi. All supporting documents must be in PDF format and apostilled/translated as required. The Immigration Service conducts background checks and may request additional documentation.

Once your residence permit is approved, you'll receive a residence permit card (oleskelulupa) or approval letter. Present this to DVV along with other registration documents, and you'll proceed with DVV registration and henkilötunnus assignment.

Registration Documents Checklist

  • Valid passport
  • Proof of residence (lease agreement, property purchase, or housing confirmation)
  • Employment contract or self-employment documentation (for non-EU citizens, the residence permit approval)
  • Birth certificate (apostilled, translated if not English)
  • Marriage/divorce certificate (if applicable, apostilled and translated)
  • Completed DVV registration form (available at dvv.fi)
  • Proof of address registration (municipal confirmation letter)

Contact your local municipal office (kunta) to find the DVV registration office serving your area. Most larger cities have dedicated immigration service points where staff speak English. Bring all documents in originals (apostilled/translated) and digital copies.

KELA Card & Social Benefits (600 words)

KELA (Kansaneläkelaitos) is Finland's Social Insurance Institution, which manages social security, unemployment benefits, child allowances, housing allowances, and health subsidies. Upon registering your henkilötunnus, you're automatically registered with KELA, and they'll mail you a KELA card to your registered address.

The KELA card is your physical proof of social insurance status. You'll present it at pharmacies (to receive medicine subsidies), when applying for unemployment benefits, when registering for healthcare, and when applying for other social support. The card typically arrives 2-3 weeks after your DVV registration is processed.

KELA benefits you may be eligible for include: health insurance (coverage for prescriptions, dental care subsidies), child allowances, unemployment benefits (if registered as a job seeker), housing allowance (if your housing costs exceed a certain percentage of your income), and student finance (if pursuing education). You can check your eligibility and apply for benefits through the online KELA portal (using your online banking login).

Tax Registration & Employment (1,000 words)

Finland's tax system is progressive and digital. Upon employment, you'll receive a tax card from Finnish tax authorities (Verohallinto) that determines your tax withholding rate. Your employer automatically withholds tax from your salary based on this card.

Tax Residency

You become tax-resident in Finland if you reside there for more than 183 days in any 12-month period or have your permanent home there. Once tax-resident, you must declare worldwide income. However, Finland has tax treaties with most countries to avoid double taxation, so you typically receive credits for taxes paid elsewhere.

Getting Your Tax Card

When you're hired, your employer will register you with tax authorities. The tax office issues a tax card (verotiedote) based on your income level. Your employer uses this to determine withholding. You can view your tax card and file taxes online through Verohallinto's portal (vero.fi)—you'll need to set up online banking or digital credentials to access it.

Annual Tax Return

Finland operates a pre-filled tax return system where your employer's reports are automatically included. In May, you'll receive your pre-filled return through the online portal. Review it for accuracy and submit it electronically. Most employed people simply approve the pre-filled return; if you have additional income sources or expenses, you'll need to add these manually.

The filing deadline is June 30. Filing is free and entirely digital through vero.fi. If you need assistance, Verohallinto offers English-language support by phone and email.

Self-Employment & Business Registration

If you're self-employed or establishing a business, you must register with the Business Register (Yritysrekisteri) before beginning operations. This registration is free and can be completed online through ytj.fi. You'll need an IBAN bank account (in your business name), a tax ID number (assigned upon registration), and documentation of your business structure.

Self-employed individuals must file monthly or quarterly VAT returns (depending on annual turnover) and annual income tax returns. Consider hiring an accountant familiar with Finnish tax law—the cost (typically 500-1,500 EUR annually for a small business) is well worth the compliance certainty.

Banking in Finland (800 words)

Opening a bank account is straightforward in Finland, but you'll need your henkilötunnus or temporary identification number to do so. Major Finnish banks include Nordea, OP (Osuuspankki), and Danske Bank. Smaller banks and online-only banks (like Wise, Revolut) also operate in Finland, though they have different requirements.

Visit a bank branch with your passport, proof of address, and your henkilötunnus (or D-number if not yet assigned). Most accounts can be opened same-day or next-day, and they come with a debit card and online banking access. Monthly fees are typically minimal (0-5 EUR) for standard checking accounts.

Online banking is standard in Finland, accessed through your bank's app or website. You'll receive personal security codes and typically need to set up two-factor authentication. Finnish banks are leaders in digital security, so expect strong authentication requirements.

For international transfers, provide your bank's SWIFT code and IBAN. Transfer costs vary by bank (typically 5-15 EUR for international transfers). For regular transfers between countries, consider specialist services like Wise that often offer better rates than traditional banks.

Healthcare Registration (800 words)

Finland has an excellent public healthcare system (terveydenhuolto) that covers all residents. Upon DVV registration, you're automatically enrolled. Your municipality assigns you a primary health center (terveysasema) where you'll register with a doctor. Healthcare is funded through taxes and is largely free or requires only small co-payments.

Your Health Center Registration

Within weeks of DVV registration, you'll receive a letter from your municipality assigning you to a health center and notifying you of your doctor. Visit the health center to complete registration and receive your health insurance documentation. This is straightforward—bring your henkilötunnus and proof of residence.

The Finnish Health Insurance System

You don't receive a physical health insurance card; your henkilötunnus serves as your health ID. Pharmacies and healthcare providers look up your coverage using your number. If you're also covered by another country's health system, inform your health center—they'll coordinate coverage to determine which system is primary.

Prescription Medications & Dental Care

Prescription medications are partially subsidized through the public system. You pay a portion (copay), and the system covers the rest, up to an annual maximum. Once you've reached the maximum out-of-pocket amount (approximately 590 EUR annually), medications are free for the rest of the year.

Routine dental care is not covered by public healthcare, only emergency dental treatment. You'll need to purchase private dental insurance or pay out-of-pocket (typically 80-200 EUR per visit). Many employers offer dental insurance as an employee benefit—check your employment package.

Property & Rental Documentation (1,000 words)

Finland's rental and property markets are well-regulated and transparent. Renting is straightforward; buying property is more complex but still manageable with proper legal guidance.

Renting in Finland

Residential leases in Finland are governed by the Rental Act (Vuokralaki). Standard residential leases are 12 months and can be terminated with one month's notice by either party at the end of the lease term. Landlords must register all residential leases with the municipality, and tenancy agreements are strongly regulated to protect tenants.

To rent, you'll typically need: valid passport or ID, proof of income (employment contract or salary history), references from previous landlords or employers, and sometimes a background check. Most landlords request a deposit (typically one month's rent) and ask that utilities be paid separately or included in rent. Read your lease carefully—it must specify rent amount, payment date, included/excluded utilities, lease term, and termination conditions.

Many rental properties are listed on Vuokraovi.com, Oikotie.fi, or Asunnot.yle.fi. Helsinki's housing market is competitive—respond quickly to listings and be prepared with documentation.

Buying Property in Finland

Buying property in Finland is more involved than renting. The process includes: property inspection, price negotiation, purchase agreement, survey and registration with the Land Registry (Kiinteistörekisteri), and formal title transfer. Non-citizens can purchase residential property without restrictions, but commercial property purchases may require permits.

The buyer typically hires a real estate lawyer to review the purchase agreement, conduct title searches, and manage registration. Lawyer fees are typically 800-1,500 EUR for a residential purchase. Mortgage lenders often recommend or require lawyer involvement.

Purchase agreements in Finland are detailed and legally binding once signed by both parties. The buyer typically has several days to complete inspections and cancel if issues are discovered. The transaction timeline is typically 4-8 weeks from offer to final ownership registration.

Essential Property Documents

  • Tenancy agreement: Must be in writing and specify all lease terms
  • Property deed & title: Official ownership documentation from Kiinteistörekisteri
  • Building survey: Property condition assessment performed by a licensed surveyor
  • Energy certificate: Required for all buildings; shows energy efficiency rating
  • Mortgage documents: If financing the purchase through a bank
  • Insurance documentation: Property insurance required for mortgaged properties
