Perintö

Definition: Finnish legal term for inheritance of property and assets from a deceased person's estate.

Overview

Perintö in Finnish law refers to inheritance—the legal transfer of a deceased person's property and assets to designated heirs. Distribution is governed by either a valid testament or Finnish statutory succession rules.

Under Finnish law, statutory succession establishes a hierarchy of heirs: spouse, children, parents, and more distant relatives. If no testament exists, the perintö is divided according to this prescribed order.

A person may accept or reject a perintö within a specified time frame. Accepting means assuming both assets and liabilities; rejecting allows avoidance of debts but forfeiture of benefits.

Finnish law provides strong protections for spouses and children, who have reserved portions (perintöosuus) that cannot be entirely removed by will. These protections ensure family members receive a minimum inheritance.

Perintö may be subject to inheritance tax (perintövero) in Finland, depending on the amount and the heir's relationship to the deceased. Tax must typically be paid before assets can be distributed.

Key Facts

  • Inheritance under Finnish law
  • Distributed by testament or statutory succession
  • Must be accepted or rejected within period
  • Subject to inheritance tax (perintövero)
  • Spouse and children have protected shares

Common Mistake

Watch out: Accepting a perintö without verifying whether debts exceed assets or understanding tax consequences.

Pro Tip

💡 Consult with a Finnish tax specialist before accepting a perintö to understand all financial obligations.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to decide whether to accept a perintö?

In Finland, heirs typically have 6 months from notification to decide whether to accept or reject the inheritance.

What is perintövero?

Perintövero is Finnish inheritance tax, levied on heirs depending on the inheritance amount and their relationship to the deceased.

Can children be completely disinherited in Finland?

No. Finnish law guarantees children a perintöosuus (reserved share) regardless of will provisions.
