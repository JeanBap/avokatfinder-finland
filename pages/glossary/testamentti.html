Testamentti

Definition: A Finnish will that specifies how a person's estate should be distributed after death.

Overview

A testamentti is a formal Finnish legal document in which a person declares how their property should be distributed upon death. It must be created with full legal capacity and clear testamentary intent, following strict formal requirements.

Finnish law recognizes both handwritten (käsinkirjoitettu testamentti) and formally witnessed wills. A handwritten testamentti must be dated and signed by the testator; a witnessed will requires signatures from two independent witnesses.

For validity, a testamentti must clearly identify the testator, specify asset distribution, and be properly signed and dated. Witness wills must be signed in the presence of both witnesses, who attest to the testator's mental capacity.

A testamentti can designate an executor (testamentinhallitsija) to manage the estate and carry out the testator's wishes. It can include specific bequests, charitable donations, and conditions on inheritances.

Finnish law protects certain heirs through reserved shares (perintöosuus), meaning spouses and children cannot be entirely disinherited. A testamentti must respect these legal protections.

Key Facts

  • Formal document declaring asset distribution
  • Can be handwritten or formally witnessed
  • Must be dated and signed by testator
  • Can designate executor and include conditions
  • Must respect reserved shares for family

Common Mistake

Watch out: Creating a testamentti that fails to meet Finnish formality requirements, or attempting to completely disinherit protected family members.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the formal requirements for a valid Finnish testamentti?

Must be dated, signed by the testator, and either handwritten or witnessed by two independent adults.

Can I modify my testamentti?

Yes, you can create a new testamentti or a written modification at any time during your lifetime.

What is a perintöosuus?

Perintöosuus is the reserved share that spouses and children receive by law, even if the will attempts to reduce their inheritance.
